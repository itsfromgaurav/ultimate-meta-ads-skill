# ad_copy
> Copywriting frameworks, tactics, templates, and compliance rules for writing high-converting Meta (Facebook/Instagram) ad copy, from *Ultimate Guide to Facebook Advertising (3rd ed.)*.

## Core frameworks

### The 7 Ad Building Blocks (Facebook Ad Builder) (Ch. 18)
A modular set of copy components you mix-and-match (Lego-style) to build winning ads, generate test variations, and reduce ad fatigue. Not every ad needs all seven, and they can run in any order (e.g., #3 opens the copy while #1 becomes the link description).

1. **Curiosity**
2. **Challenge / Frustration / Pain or Desire**
3. **Benefits and Desires** — surface the deep desire by appending *"so you can"* / *"so that"* to any benefit. Speak to the conversation in the prospect's mind, not the product features (e.g., Samcart sells checkout software but the ad sells "doubling your sales"). Benefit claims can trip Facebook Ad Policy — read the policy carefully.
4. **Credibility or Authority Statement** — why should people listen to, trust, and obey you? Inherited/borrowed credibility counts (e.g., airline experience used for a checklist metaphor). Avoid sounding boastful.
5. **Give an "Aha Moment"**
6. **Call-to-Action (CTA)** — make the next action crystal clear; people are skimming. For video ads ~3 min+, get at least a soft CTA in the first 60–90 seconds.
7. **Overcoming Objections (mini-FAQ)** — a mini-FAQ placed after the first CTA increases conversions, trust, and relevance scores even when it lengthens the ad. "It flat out works."

*How to use it:* Brainstorm as many options as possible for each block on a notepad/whiteboard first, building a deep inventory of components to assemble and test quickly. Automated tool: www.PerryMarshall.com/fbtools.

### The 13 Elements of Persuasive Ad Copy (Ralph Burns / Dominate Web Media)
Copywriting "seasonings" to spice up copy. Use sparingly — you do NOT need all of them in every ad. They reinforce hooks and offers; ad copy plays a **supporting** role to a great video. (Curiosity was the original 14th element, now covered in Ch. 18.)

1. **Be Authentic** — write in your own voice; don't copy verbatim. Authentic AND effective beats merely effective. Call out the avatar and a pain point (Policy-compliant).
2. **Simplify** — one word instead of two or three; prefer one-syllable words; write low reading level. (50% of US adults can't read at an 8th-grade level. Trump spoke at ~5th-grade level in 2016.) Big words hurt conversions and scale.
3. **Write for Homer** (Simpson), not Hawking — clear, concise, cut-to-the-chase at ~5th-grade level to "stop the thumb scroll."
4. **Show Proof** — for cold audiences, insert credibility statements throughout, each tied to a pain point (usually longer copy). Make the incredible credible.
5. **Ask Questions**
6. **Answer Immediately** — give the answer on the next line, or tell them exactly what to do next. People want to be led. "Show people you can help them by actually helping them."
7. **Use Revealing Words** — words implying discovery/novelty (Discover, hidden, astonishing) but NOT hyperbole. Keep adjectives minimal; action verbs over adjectives ≥2:1. Avoid superlatives (best, finest, outstanding).
8. **"You" Rules** — second person creates possession/ownership and captures attention; default to it. NEVER tie "you" to a personal attribute (Policy violation). Don't overuse it (creepy).
9. **Non-Standard Callouts** — frame novel/important points with brackets or parentheses, e.g., `[Special Discount]`, `[Don't miss your opportunity]`.
10. **Capitalize** — capitalize only the ONE word/phrase to remember (e.g., "FREE TRAINING"). Never all-caps everything (scammy; Policy notices).
11. **Embrace the Ellipsis** — create a broken thought needing resolution on the next line; underscores scarcity/importance (e.g., "click here to get your FREE copy . . . while they last").
12. **Mix It Up** — split-test copy lengths (see Copy Length below).
13. **Employ Emojis** — punctuate benefits and the main message; don't overdo it. Split tests: higher CTR, higher Relevance Score, lower CPC, lower CPA.

### UPSYD — Audience Awareness Copy Structures (Ch. 13 / Flight Plan)
Match copy depth and structure to where the prospect sits on the awareness ladder (Unaware → Problem-aware → Solution-aware → Your-product-aware → most-aware-Deal).

- **Long-copy educational ad (Steps 2–3, Problem/Solution Aware):** (1) Contrarian "Big Aha" hook question; (2) Provide value — debunk myths; (3) Introduce the solution; (4) Provide further proof; (5) Pivot into the pitch. Goal: move people from awareness to intent.
- **Long-copy problem-aware ad (Step 1–2, Unaware/Problem-aware):** scary problem hook + study stat → show study results → transition to how/why you built the solution → scarcity offer → clear CTA.
- **Short-copy solution-aware ad (Step 2–3):** free-plus-shipping offer + scarcity + cost-reframe + social-cause framing + share request. Simpler because they already know the problem and that solutions exist.

### The 7 Power Questions (Ryan Deiss) (Ch. 20)
Self-check questions to run before finishing any copy. Single underlying goal: **FOCUSED ATTENTION** — if people don't focus, it won't convert. Answering even one or two well produces far more powerful copy and beats most competitors.

1. How do I make the offer appear NOVEL, UNIQUE, DISTINCTIVE (new)?
2. How do I make the offer SIMPLE and EASY to understand? (When people grasp something quickly, they like it more AND ascribe more validity to it — "if I understand it, it must be true." Use rhythm/rhyme. Simplifying means pulling back on claims and not explaining every nuance.)
3. What OPENING QUESTION, when answered, triggers a desire for consistency and drives action?
4. How do I PRE-EXPOSE the audience to a concept linked to a desired emotional stimulus?
5. What MENTAL LINKS and ASSOCIATIONS (memory, nostalgia, identity) can I tap and tie to the offer?
6. How can OPEN LOOPS hold attention and leverage the close for cognitive closure? (Zeigarnik effect — weave the sales message mid-story and let story closure land AT the point of sale; closing the story too early switches off attention.)
7. How do I create a visual/mental PORTAL the prospect passes through that opens them to new opportunities?

## Tactics & best practices

- **Long-copy and video ads produce higher-quality, more-qualified leads** (the Keto ad was ~1,000 words). They pre-frame prospects for the next step. "Cheap leads don't run businesses. Buyers run businesses." Ideally pay MORE per lead than competitors — qualified leads convert. (Ch. 16)
- **Ogilvy's reading chain:** each element exists to pull the reader into the next (headline → subhead → body → CTA). The first line must compel reading all the way to the CTA. (Ch. 16)
- **Always use the Call-to-Action button** on the ad, not just a URL. (Ch. 28)
- **Tell a story with a fictitious person, not "you"** — "you" can feel like calling people out and scare them. Use a character (e.g., "Jeff" in the Kabbage carousel); the reader still pictures themselves. Carousel ads work well for before/after storytelling. (Ch. 21)
- **Like-ad rules:** body copy capped at 90 characters; headlines can't be changed (body is the only editable text); check the mobile feed. Honest CTA: "Click Like to join." Don't make promises in exchange for the Like. (Ch. 11)
- **Fix a low Relevance Score = fix message-to-market match:** (1) confirm the offer is what people actually want; (2) rewrite copy to hit their biggest pains, desires, and fears; (3) move the prospect from undesirable BEFORE state to desirable AFTER state. (Troubleshooting)

### Copy length — Mix It Up (split-test 3–4 versions)
- **Ultra-short:** one or maybe two lines.
- **Short:** ~3–5 lines — pairs with strong video ads (the video does the selling).
- **Long:** 5+ lines — image ads selling a core product or consult call; cold-traffic big asks.
- **Long-ass:** up to a thousand-plus words — heavy persuasion for big cold-traffic asks.

Guidance: a solid selling video needs LESS copy; image ads selling a core product/consult call need LONGER copy; long copy fits big asks for cold traffic.

## Checklists

### Pre-publish copy check (7 Power Questions, Ch. 20)
- [ ] Does the offer appear novel, unique, and distinctive?
- [ ] Is the offer simple and easy to understand?
- [ ] Is there an opening question that triggers consistency/action?
- [ ] Have I pre-exposed the audience to a desired emotional concept?
- [ ] Have I tapped mental links/associations (memory, nostalgia, identity)?
- [ ] Have I opened a loop to hold attention until the close?
- [ ] Have I created a mental/visual portal to new opportunity?

### Testimonial-gathering script (5 questions, Ch. 15)
- [ ] What problem/pain/frustration were they trying to solve before your product?
- [ ] What were they worried/hesitant about before buying? (overcomes objections)
- [ ] What were their results? How is life/business better now? (transformation)
- [ ] What did they like best about [your product]?
- [ ] Would they recommend it to a friend? If so, how can it help them?

## Copy / templates

**Long-copy problem-aware ad (toxic water bottle):**
```
Are your water bottles leaking cancer-causing toxins into the water
you or your kids are drinking? New study reveals [really scary statistic
goes here].

[Show the study results — problem awareness.]
[Transition: how/why you created the toxin-free solution that also makes
water taste amazing and be healthier.]
[Special pricing/promotion framed by your mission to save lives —
"until supplies last."]

To try this amazing water before supplies run out, click
www.AmazingWatersXYZ.com.
```

**Short-copy solution-aware free-plus-shipping ad:**
```
Award-winning Amazing Water XYZ is saving the world one bottle at a
time . . . and to celebrate, they are giving away some of their water
for free! (You just have to pay a few dollars for shipping. Don't worry,
you can think about those measly few bucks that you didn't spend on a
Big Mac as money that went to making the world a better place.)
Click here to get yours free, while supplies last. (And please share
this post if you want to spread the love.)
www.AmazingWaterXYZ.com/free.
```
*Techniques: free-plus-shipping, scarcity, cost reframe (Big Mac), social-cause framing, share request for organic reach.*

**Consistency opener ("Is your product good?"):**
```
Let me ask you a question. Is your product or service good? Does it
work? If you put it in their hands, if you put it in front of the right
person, will they get a good response? If you answered yes to that
question, then I want to show you how to sell a whole lot more of it.
```

**Like-ad CTA:**
```
Click Like to join
```

**Carousel story (fictitious "Jeff" — Kabbage App):**
```
Jeff wants to grow his business. He downloads the Kabbage App to get a
line of credit up to 100 grand... He applies first thing in the
morning... [Jeff withdrawing funds on his way to work] → CTA
```

## Pitfalls to avoid

- **Don't use direct claims/promises about the audience** — Facebook bans them. "Lose 30 lbs in 30 Days" gets banned; share your OWN results instead ("how I lost 28 lbs in six weeks"). Testimonials work, but some niches have rules (e.g., no before/after photos in weight loss). (Ch. 13)
- **Don't tie "you" to a personal attribute** — "Do you want to lose weight?", "Do you have diabetes?", "Do you want a thicker fuller beard?" all violate Policy. Compliant: "superfoods are some of the highest vibing food you will ever come across." (Element 8)
- **Don't overdo all-caps** — looks scammy and Facebook Policy notices; capitalize only the one key thing. (Element 10)
- **Don't exaggerate / use hyperbole** — revealing words are overdone online; hyperbole repels more customers than it attracts. Avoid superlatives. (Element 7)
- **Don't copy others' copy verbatim** — quick but ineffective; authenticity wins. (Element 1)
- **Don't make the next step unclear** — an ad with high social shares wasn't converting until copy was revamped with a strong offer/CTA and a URL added to the copy; then it converted "like crazy." Always make the next step in the journey clear. (Troubleshooting)
- **Watch benefit-driven statements** — they can violate Facebook's stringent ad policy and get ads disapproved; read the Ad Policy section. (Block #3)
- **Don't close the story too early** — if cognitive closure happens before the sale, the brain switches off (Zeigarnik / "waiter who forgot the order"). Weave the message mid-story; close the story at the point of sale. (Ch. 20)
