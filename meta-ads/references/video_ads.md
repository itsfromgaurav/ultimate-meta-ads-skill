# video_ads

> How to plan, produce, and deploy Facebook/Instagram video ads that move cold audiences to purchase — the Three-Step Video Ad Formula (Stop the Scroll → EDIE → CTA), EDIE content types, the UPSYD ascension play, video offer patterns, benchmarks, and pitfalls.

## Core frameworks

### Three-Step Video Ad Formula
The agency's (Dominate Web Media) core formula for building long-term, high-ROI Facebook video ads. Applied to every client video; backed by 600+ million paid video views of internal data. (Three-Step Video Ad Formula chapter)

1. **Stop the Scroll** — Grab attention in the first 3–10 seconds.
2. **Use the EDIE Formula** — Educate, Demonstrate, Inform, or Entertain (include at least one).
3. **Call to Action** — Tell the viewer exactly what to do next, often multiple times.

The formula is flexible: there is no fixed required video length. Content must match your brand and product; everything else is fluid. Best performers have ranged from **20 seconds to 18 minutes**.

### EDIE Formula (Step 2)
EDIE = **E**ducate, **D**emonstrate, **I**nform, or **E**ntertain — a simple way to ensure at least one of Berger's virality principles is present in every video ad. Include at least one (not all four) so the video isn't just *seen* but watched, enjoyed, remembered, and shared. (Three-Step Video Ad Formula chapter)

- **Educate** — Teach / give tips. You don't even have to be on camera (a PowerPoint/Keynote screen recording works). Don't give a full webinar; give value plus at least one "Aha!" moment / nugget that reveals the value of your offer, then say they'll find more after completing the CTA. If you give value, they'll share it and obey the CTA.
- **Demonstrate** — Show the product in action. Works great for technology brands. "We all love to see gadgets, and we love to see them work." Showing it work makes viewers want their own.
- **Inform** — Goes further than Educate: instead of delivering a skill, give info the viewer didn't know before. Useful when the product needs little education but has features people should know about.
- **Entertain** — Hardest to do well. Example: The Dollar Beard Club pitched the simplest product the simplest way — a parody of a Dollar Shave Club video.

### UPSYD — Selling cold to the top of the staircase with one video ad
It is possible to move broad cold audiences from the bottom to the top of the UPSYD staircase and sell a physical product with a single ad, by combining the **Three-Step Video Ad Formula** with the **UPSYD framework**, plus retargeting to maximize ROI. (Ch. 13)

- Take the same hook and copy from your winning long-copy ad and turn it into a video.
- Video is a great medium to build trust, make connections, offer value, and build brand — so it's much easier to ascend all five UPSYD steps in a very small amount of time.

### Authentic video formula (avatar + pain point + personality)
"The exact formula that we use for nearly all our videos." (The 13 Elements of Persuasive Ad Copy)

1. Call out your **avatar** directly.
2. Call out a **pain point** in a Policy-compliant way.
3. Express the customer's **individuality / authentic voice** in the ad copy.
4. **Reinforce all of it with the video itself** — ideally the customer appears IN the video as the voice of the business.

The copy doesn't have to pose a question; it can simply get the prospect thinking about a shared problem, then instruct them what to do next (watch the video, then click the link for more tips).

## Tactics & best practices

### Where video fits in the plan
- **Validate the message first, then make the video.** You can get great results without video as long as you follow the proven ad formula; a winning long-copy ad de-risks video by validating the message in text form first. (Ch. 3)
- **Video is introduced in Phase 3 (last) of the Facebook Flight Plan.** Video is powerful but resource-intensive (storyboard, script, film, edit). In the first two phases you're still finding the hook/offer that resonates — far simpler/faster to test by changing text and image than by remaking a whole video. (Ch. 15)
- **Turn your best long-copy ad into a video script.** Repurpose your top-performing long-copy ad as the basic script. But if you find it easier to make videos than write long copy, start with video out of the gate (sometimes faster than writing out the story). (Ch. 15)

### Production & sourcing
- **Mine existing assets first.** Some of the best-performing campaigns were video ads made from videos clients already had on YouTube. The first thing the agency CEO (Ralph Burns) does with a new client is watch every video on their YouTube channel. Leverage existing assets rather than always producing new. (Ch. 15)
- **Don't over-produce testimonials.** Testimonial videos (or image ads using screenshots) are authentic proof and help prospects believe they can get a similar result — but if it's too perfect/polished, people trust it less than something that looks shot on an iPhone. (Ch. 15)
- **Invest in the idea, not production value.** The Dollar Beard Club put all initial energy/investment into creativity; a ~$700 video generated millions. (Three-Step Video Ad Formula chapter)

### Educate before you sell (cold traffic)
- When running to people unaware of you or your solution, **build authority, credibility, and rapport by educating first, deliver upfront value, then pivot at the end** to the next step. (Ch. 17)
- A long, highly actionable **ungated** video (no opt-in) can lead **directly to a purchase CTA** instead of a gated lead magnet — it proves it can help the prospect by actually helping them, then leads them to buy more of what they were just shown. (Ch. 16)

### Hooks & pre-framing
- **Transformation as a hook.** Show a real before/after process on video (e.g., flipping a $40k house sold for $90k); give practical info, build credibility/rapport, and pre-frame the prospect so the paid offer (the missing ingredient — e.g., the money to fund the flip) becomes the logical next step. Work backward from the offer to the hook. (Ch. 16)
- **Video before a lead magnet to pre-frame the next-step sale.** Normally you don't need a video for a low-commitment lead magnet, but a video at first contact builds rapport/credibility and subconsciously pre-commits prospects to the product sold on the thank-you page — "where the magic happens." (Ch. 17)

### Attention & motion
- **Add motion to catch the eye.** Small video ads or GIFs catch attention because they move (e.g., a gauge that actually moves; a 9-second Messenger ad where a text bubble appears like a real-time conversation; battery progressing from green to nearly dead over a campaign). Motion catches the eye while still conveying the message. (Ch. 21)
- **Audio is more important than video.** What enters through the ears stays in the mind ~5 seconds; what enters the eyes is gone in under a second. A picture is only "worth a thousand words" if paired with a memorable sound (like a jingle). Matters even more since Facebook (Feb 2017) began autoplaying sound-on in the feed (sound fades in/out as users scroll, if phone sound is on). (Three-Step Video Ad Formula chapter)

### Portals — reset attention and prime a decision (7 Power Questions, Q7)
A "portal" moves the prospect (visually, mentally, or via audio) from one place/state to another, resetting attention so they're more open to a decision or change. Without a portal, the audience is less likely to make a change. (Ch. 20)

- **Visual** — the classic product-launch shot of the marketer talking to you while driving in his car (you "ride along," passing place to place).
- **Walk-through** — a local plumber video starts outside: "Hi, I'm Fred… come inside, I want you to meet some people," camera follows him through the door.
- **Auditory** — change/start background music as the transition occurs (visual-auditory portal).
- Portals can be subtle (a background color change) or obvious. Iconic examples: Dorothy's doorway shifting black-and-white to color in *Wizard of Oz* (with music starting as the door opens); the wedding aisle (enter separately, exit married back through the same portal). The cue signals "what was before is not the same anymore; it's okay to act on a new path."

### Campaign mechanics
- **Use the Video Views objective to build retargetable engaged audiences.** It gets your video in front of people most likely to actually watch, and lets you build audiences of viewers to retarget along the customer journey (unaware → customer). A 5-min video to a most-likely-to-watch audience can earn a few thousand views in days; if half watch half, that's ~1,000 people who spent 2.5 minutes focused on your brand. Powerful for launching a product/offer to a COLD audience. (Ch. 26)

## Checklists

### Before you publish a video ad
- [ ] First 3–10 seconds stop the scroll
- [ ] At least one EDIE element present (Educate, Demonstrate, Inform, or Entertain)
- [ ] Audio is strong/memorable (assume sound-on autoplay)
- [ ] Avatar is called out directly
- [ ] A pain point is called out in a Policy-compliant way
- [ ] The customer's authentic voice / individuality comes through (ideally customer appears on camera)
- [ ] Educates and delivers upfront value BEFORE pivoting to sell (for cold traffic)
- [ ] A portal (visual/auditory) is used to reset attention and prime the decision
- [ ] At least one clear CTA — and multiple CTAs across a long video
- [ ] On videos ~3 min+, a soft CTA appears within the first 60–90 seconds
- [ ] CTA(s) are authentic to the brand, "seeding the next thing" (not a hard "buy now" infomercial)
- [ ] Video length matches the brand/product (no arbitrary length rule)
- [ ] Checked the client's existing YouTube channel for reusable footage

### Working backward from the offer (hook design)
- [ ] Define the end business goal (e.g., get prospects on a call / to purchase)
- [ ] Identify the offer's "missing ingredient" the video pre-frames
- [ ] Derive the hook from the offer (e.g., hook "buy a house with no money" → checklist offer "15 things to flip your first house," one of which is you need funding)

## Copy/templates

Video CTA tone — keep it natural and authentic, always seeding the next step:

```
It's a marathon, not a late-night "buy now while stocks last" infomercial.
Place a soft CTA ~90 seconds in, then ~4 CTAs total across a 9-minute video.
```

Avatar + pain-point opener (copy need not be a question):

```
Get the prospect thinking about a problem they share, then tell them
exactly what to do next: "Watch the video, then click the link for more tips."
```

Plumber walk-through portal (auditory/visual transition):

```
"Hi, I'm Fred... come inside, I want you to meet some people."
(camera follows him through the door into the store)
```

## Pitfalls to avoid

- **Don't run a video that's just a straight "buy now" CTA to cold traffic.** "If you're thinking about just putting up a video with a straight call to action to buy your product, don't do it because it won't work. There has to be a whole lot more to it than just a CTA." Educate and give value first, then pivot to sell. (Ch. 17)
- **Don't forget the CTA — and don't bury it only at the end.** Many clients skip the CTA thinking social is "all about branding and engagement." Branding matters, but you must tell viewers what to do, multiple times, since only a portion watch a long video to the end. (Three-Step chapter)
- **Don't obey arbitrary video-length rules.** Beware experts who insist a video "must be" 30 sec or 5 min — they can rationalize either way. A proven formula with a great track record is not always *your* formula. Match content to your brand and product; everything else is fluid. (Three-Step chapter)
- **Don't over-produce testimonials/proof videos.** Too polished reads as less trustworthy than something that looks shot on an iPhone. (Ch. 15)
- **Don't skip the portal.** Without a visual/auditory portal to reset attention, the audience is less likely to make a change. (Ch. 20)
