# hooks_messaging
> How to craft Meta (Facebook/Instagram) ad hooks and marketing messages that match audience awareness, trigger curiosity/desire, and convert — from the *Ultimate Guide to Facebook Advertising (3rd ed.)*.

## Core frameworks

### UPSYD — Awareness Staircase (Ch. 13, 16)
A five-step awareness continuum used to match every hook and ad to where the prospect currently sits:
- **U** — Unaware (bottom step): completely unaware of the problem.
- **P** — Problem Aware: knows the problem, not the solution.
- **S** — Solution Aware: knows solutions/companies exist (the "low-hanging fruit").
- **Y** — Your-Solution Aware: knows your specific solution/product name.
- **D** — Deal (top step): ready to buy on an offer.

Steps and how to use them:
1. Picture giant stairs. Moving up one step is easy; skipping a step is harder; jumping 3–4 steps at once is impossible — it becomes a leap, not a step.
2. Use **targeting** to figure out which step the audience is on, then craft messaging to move them up the remaining steps.
3. **Key tradeoff:** the higher you start, the more directly you can go for the sale — but the audience is smaller. The lower you start, the more value/education you must provide — but it scales far better because you can target large, broad audiences and let Facebook's algorithm work.
4. This contradicts "the riches are in the niches": niche down *within the ad's content*, but target big and broad for better long-term results.
5. Copy fit: Unaware/Problem-aware → long-copy ad that makes them aware of the problem, then transitions to your solution. Solution-aware → simpler short-copy ad referencing the known problem.
6. A generic solution-name hook (e.g., "how to flip houses") works only on Solution-aware prospects (small segment → scalability problem). To reach broader/unaware audiences, use a more specific or curiosity-driven hook. (See Ad Builder, Ch. 18.)

### STEPPS — Six Principles of Sharing (Jonah Berger, *Contagious: Why Things Catch On*) (Ch. 13)
Build hooks that leverage as many of these as possible to maximize shares (which the algorithm rewards):
- **S — Social Currency:** we share things that make us look good.
- **T — Triggers:** top of mind is tip of tongue (e.g., a breakfast hook re-triggers every time someone sits down to eat breakfast).
- **E — Emotions:** when we care, we share.
- **P — Public Visibility:** when something is built to show, it's built to grow.
- **P — Practical Value:** something the audience can actually use.
- **S — Stories:** information shared in an entertaining manner.

### 7 Power Questions — selected hook questions (Ch. 20)
A checklist for knowing when copy is ready. Hook-relevant questions:
- **Q1 — Make the offer novel, unique, and distinctive.** Humans are biologically wired to give focused attention to the unfamiliar (survival instinct). If the offer isn't actually new, ask: (1) How can it *feel* new and noteworthy? (2) How can it be *made* new? (3) What's a different angle? (4) Are there current news stories to attach it to? Proof: "Major announcement" is one of the best email open-rate subject lines; cable news exploits this with "Breaking News" banners.
- **Q3 — Open with a question that triggers commitment & consistency** (Cialdini, *Pre-Suasion*, 2016). General questions engage the mind, not identity. Better questions make prospects affirm something about *themselves* (Are you brave? helpful?), then point that self-image at the desired action. Example: "Do you consider yourself brave?" → "Yes" → "Then are you brave enough to end one of the biggest schisms in business between sales and marketing?" Caution: choose questions whose honest "yes" *qualifies* the prospect; can become manipulative if pushed too far.

### 13 Elements of Persuasive Ad Copy — Element 5: Ask Questions (Ch. "The 13 Elements")
- Open the **first line of post copy** with a question that addresses your hook, then **restate that question as a benefit statement in the headline** — same theme, two ways, two places.
- The first line of post copy is the most important line in a Facebook ad (it's the first words seen in the newsfeed).
- Example: Question (first line) = "Looking for an easy way to get more superfoods?" → Headline = "Get all your healthy superfoods in one drink." Reuse the same ad for cold traffic, retargeting, image ads, and slideshow ads.

### Three-Step Video Ad Formula — Step 1: Stop the Scroll (Ch. "Three-Step Video Ad Formula")
The first **3–10 seconds** are vital (autoplay sound-on is an opportunity, not a guarantee). Three proven attention tactics:
1. **Blatant hand motions** — wave at the viewer, pull them toward you like a store demonstrator. Not subtle, but it works.
2. **Text overlay / captions** — Facebook can auto-add subtitles; catches sound-off viewers and (since we read faster than we speak) delivers the message faster.
3. **Pattern interrupt** — make the picture look unusual (same principle as red warning signs / black-and-white crosswalks): movement, an extreme close-up, or a selfie-style look. The Squatty Potty pooping-unicorn video is an extreme example; you don't need anything outrageous.

### Facebook Ad Builder — 7 copy building blocks (selected) (Ch. 18)
- **#1 Curiosity** — curiosity-based hooks grab attention and keep people reading/watching. Techniques: ask a thought-provoking question, use a shocking statement, or contradict conventional wisdom.
- **#2 Challenge / Frustration / Pain / Desire** — call out the audience's biggest challenge or desire in the opening line, sourced **verbatim from surveys**. Use two deep-dive survey questions (see Copy/templates). Ryan Levesque's *Ask* (Ch. 13 survey) and Glen Livingston's survey/quiz material are references.
- **#5 Give an "Aha Moment"** — reveal an insight or expose a common belief as a myth/inaccurate/recently-changed. Builds instant trust, makes the ad far more shareable, and is HUGE for the algorithm (cheaper clicks/impressions/views, more impressions, higher ROI).

## Tactics & best practices

**Hook fundamentals**
- A **hook is your marketing message** — the front-facing message prospects see before becoming customers; it intrigues them and is the primary reason they take your offer (Ch. 16). Analogy: customer = fish, hook = the marketing message, offer/bait = the offer. The hook is a "mental appetizer." A great Facebook ad always starts with a great hook.
- **Hook ≠ Offer** (Ch. 16, 21). The offer is the action you want (download lead magnet, buy product); the hook is how you sell/market it. **One offer can have ten hooks** if testing ten messages. Creative should convey the *hook*, not just show the product.
- **Lead with value and a quick win before selling** (Ch. 4). Deliver an immediate "aha moment" and a real quick win first; only *then* make the offer. (James the guitarist: prove four chords play almost any song, give a volunteer a 5-minute win, *then* offer.)

**Curiosity & knowledge-gap hooks**
- **Knowledge Gap hook** (Ch. 16): create a gap in the prospect's knowledge — an itch they must scratch by reading on. Best when combined with a counter-intuitive statement (e.g., "Is breakfast REALLY the Most Important Meal of the Day?").
- **Big Aha Moment hook** (Ch. 13): pair something counterintuitive/against-conventional-wisdom with genuinely valuable content to drive shares (e.g., "Is breakfast really the most important meal of the day, or is it the most important meal that should be skipped?").
- **Curiosity + Education hook** (Ch. 16): trigger curiosity *and* teach a new possibility (e.g., "build your first digital product" beats "start your own business and leave your dead-end job"). Works best for solution-aware audiences; to scale to unaware audiences, tie it back to the deeper desire (freedom/lifestyle/money).

**Desire- and pain-driven hooks**
- **Speak to the want, not the need** (Ch. 16): hook with what they emotionally *want* (play their favorite song), deliver what they *need* in the paid product.
- **Pair emotionally-charged desire + a specific, measurable outcome** (Ch. 16): e.g., "drive the ball ten yards farther" beats vague benefits because it taps ego/bragging rights *and* promises a defined result.
- **Specificity + immediacy** (Ch. 16): deliver a super-specific, immediate result, not a broad/long-term one (e.g., "how to sell from the stage" beats "build a six-figure coaching practice"). Find the one tiny thing that compels the click.
- **Move toward desire** (Ch. 16): ask the 3 desire questions, then repeatedly ask "why do they really want this?" to reach the *desire behind the desire*, then identify the end benefit.
- **Move away from pain (loss aversion)** (Ch. 16): people do more to avoid pain than gain pleasure (Robbins); losses are ~2× as powerful as equivalent gains (Tversky & Kahneman — better not to lose $5 than to find $5). Ask the 3 pain questions, then embed your solution inside the hook and cure it with your offer.
- **Embed the solution inside the specific hook** (Ch. 16): a great hook is specific to a narrow avatar with the solution embedded in the problem it agitates (e.g., "how to reliably track your Facebook leads, sales, and ROI for your customers" beats generic "how to get results with Facebook").

**Targeting message to the person**
- **Match message to awareness level (UPSYD)** — don't lead with the solution name to broad audiences (see Pitfalls).
- **Different angles for different buyer personalities** (Ch. 15): within any audience hit (1) "I want the best" (competitive/egotistical), (2) impulse buyers, (3) process-driven/methodical/logic-based, (4) gut-feel/emotion-based people — each with different messaging, ad types, angles, and hooks.
- **Three Hooks / Three Headlines / Three Videos** (Ch. 17): use three of each instead of one to tap multiple specific success drivers rather than betting on a single angle (Michael Smart's PR training: promised a 24-hour boost on reporter pitches).

**Creative that reflects the hook (Ch. 21)**
- **Creative must convey the hook**, not just show the product or "something that looks good."
- **Play off emotions/associations already in the culture**: borrow pre-loaded emotion from icons people already relate to (low iPhone battery or low bank account → portrays scarcity).

**Research methods**
- **Google the hook** (Ch. 16): search a question keyword (e.g., "intermittent fasting questions" → ~500k results signals interest), read FAQ/expert articles for contrarian angles, search a problem phrase, use the Google suggestion tool to surface the most popular searches as hook roots. Borrow the hook without plagiarizing (as Ogilvy did from Pierce-Arrow). Essential when you don't know the market/avatar; still uncovers unknown pains/desires for known markets.
- **Survey for hooks** (Building Block #2, Ch. 18): pull opening lines verbatim from deep-dive survey responses (responses are often nearly identical with slight variation).

## Checklists

**Why the Ketogenic Living hook works — 4 criteria** (Ch. 16) — "Is breakfast really the most important meal of the day?":
- [ ] Curiosity-based and contrarian.
- [ ] Shatters a conventional myth held by millions (that breakfast is the most important meal).
- [ ] Educates/informs on something new and interesting.
- [ ] Speaks to the avatar's pain point (never has time to eat breakfast, feels guilty about it).

**A great offer is** (Ch. 16):
- [ ] Highly specific.
- [ ] Easily consumable.
- [ ] Provides immediate results when implemented.
- [ ] Speaks to a known desire.
- [ ] Incomplete relative to the end product (leaves them wanting the paid product).

**Move Toward Desire — 3 desire questions** (Ch. 16):
- [ ] What are our prospect's desires?
- [ ] What does our prospect want more than anything else?
- [ ] What do we have in our product that moves them toward that one thing they want most? (Then repeatedly ask "why do they really want this?" → desire behind the desire.)

**Move Away From Pain — 3 pain questions** (Ch. 16):
- [ ] What are your target market's greatest problems? (What keeps them up at night?)
- [ ] What are they most afraid of?
- [ ] Why do they want to change? (Then embed your solution in the hook and cure it with your offer.)

## Copy/templates

**Desire + Benefit Hook Formula** (Ch. 16):
```
They want [specific desire] so that they can [benefit].
```
> [specific desire] = what they want more than anything else; [benefit] = why they want it / the end benefit of fulfilling that desire. Write out about a dozen of these and keep going (even if bad at first) until you find the perfect match.

**If/Then Hook Statement** (Ch. 16):
```
If only [solution]... Then [benefit].
```
> Example: "If only there was a reliable system out there that would simplify all the setup and analysis. Then that would allow me to take on more customers." The "if" reveals the solution (or desire/pain); the "then" reveals the benefit. Make sure the solution you agitate is embedded in your own offer/cheat sheet. Derived hook: "how to reliably track your Facebook leads, sales, and ROI for your customers."

**Question-first-line → benefit-headline pattern** (Element 5, Ch. 13 Elements):
```
First line of post copy: Looking for an easy way to get more superfoods?
Headline:               Get all your healthy superfoods in one drink.
```

**Identity / commitment-consistency opener** (Q3 / survey case study, Ch. 20):
```
Pardon me — do you consider yourself to be a helpful person?
Great! If so, I can really use your help...
```
```
Do you consider yourself brave?  →  Then are you brave enough to end one of
the biggest schisms in business between sales and marketing?
```

**Novelty hook copy** (Ch. 20):
```
Major announcement
```
> One of the best email open rates; works because it signals the new/unfamiliar (survival instinct). TV equivalent: the "Breaking News" banner.

**Two deep-dive survey questions for Building Block #2** (Ch. 18):
```
(1) Frustration/Challenge: What's your number-one single biggest challenge or
frustration with using Facebook to drive traffic and sales right now? (Please be
as detailed and specific as possible. Go beyond saying "too confusing" or "too
expensive." The more specific and detailed you are, the more likely I'll be able
to cover your topic.)

(2) Desire: Why do you want to grow your business using Facebook ads or any
online channel? What is the biggest reason why you want to master this or have
someone master it for you?
```
> Example opening line pulled verbatim: "scaling your business with max ROI and minimum wasted money."

**Desire-behind-the-desire ad copy examples** (Van Halen avatar, Ch. 16):
```
Want to play those Van Halen songs you've always wanted to play ever since you
were in high school?
Learn how to play like Eddie Van Halen!
Struggling to play Eruption and can't get the fingering down?
...so you can impress your friends / steal the show at the next family reunion.
```

## Pitfalls to avoid
- **Don't frame skill as an intimidating mountain** (Ch. 4): emphasizing massive effort (thousands of hours, the 10,000-hour mark, rare natural talent) makes prospects feel success is untouchable, so they quit before starting. Instead lower the perceived barrier — "you just had a little help learning some shortcuts" — and make them believe they can do it too.
- **Don't lead with the solution name to broad audiences** (Ch. 13): a search-style hook like "Download your FREE Intermittent Fasting cheat sheet now!" only lands on Your-Solution-Aware audiences. On broad/less-aware audiences the term may even repel (e.g., "fasting" read as a religious ritual) and the message goes over their heads — they need education first.
- **Don't use a generic solution-name hook when you need scale** (Ch. 16): e.g., "how to flip houses" resonates only with the small solution-aware segment → scalability problem. Use a more specific/curiosity-driven hook to reach the larger, less-aware market.
- **Don't lead with credentials or a discount** (Ch. 4): give aha moments, build relationship, and motivate consumption first — rather than opening with your credentials or a hard sell.
- **Don't make the creative just a pretty product shot** (Ch. 21): if the image/video doesn't convey the *hook*, it won't carry a high-converting campaign (e.g., the report-card-"F" image beat the lead-magnet mockup).
