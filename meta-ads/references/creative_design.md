# creative_design

> How to conceive, build, and direct high-converting Meta (Facebook/Instagram) ad creative — image/video concepting, hook-to-image translation, design execution, formats, placements, and the psychology of stopping the scroll. Source: *Ultimate Guide to Facebook Advertising (3rd ed.)*.

## Core frameworks

### 7 Power Questions — Q4: Pre-expose the audience to an emotional stimulus (Ch. 20)
Before the ask, plant a concept (in the image or words) that triggers the emotional state required for the purchase decision — even if that concept is logically unrelated to the offer.
1. Identify the **desired emotional stimulus**: how do you want the audience to feel in order to buy? What emotional state is required for the final purchase decision?
2. Pre-expose them to a concept that evokes that emotion *before* the offer.
3. The trigger concept can be completely unrelated to the product — it works purely by priming the emotion (this is why perfume/cologne ads "sell sex": romance/desire is the emotional stimulus that drives the purchase).

Supporting evidence:
- **Valentine Street pre-suasion experiment** (Cialdini, *Pre-Suasion*): Control — an attractive young woman asks a middle-aged man to help retrieve her phone from a group of "ruffians"; most men decline. Test — a first woman asks "Can I get directions to Valentine Street?" (Valentine = a real local street, romance concept), then a second woman immediately asks for help; willingness to intervene jumped dramatically. Re-test with "Main Street" (unlinked concept) reverted results to the low control level. Merely pre-exposing to a concept linked to the desired emotion changed behavior.
- **DigitalMarketer low-battery ad** (Fig. 20.1): promoted an early-bird sale about to end; image was the iPhone low-battery warning (~10% red sliver). The dead-battery image has nothing logically to do with the event selling out, but it pre-exposes viewers to nervous energy/urgency ("OMG, I've got to plug it in"), transferring that urgency to the offer.

### 7 Power Questions — Q5: Tap mental links, associations, and nostalgia (Ch. 20)
Where Q4 targets emotion, Q5 targets **memory and identity** — a deeper feeling. Thought itself occurs by association (we fear something because it reminds us of an earlier hurt).
1. Ask: what nostalgia, metaphor, common shared experience, or memory can be positively associated with the offer?
2. Tap a whole group's shared memories/feelings, then attach the product to them.
3. Use **metaphors** (associating one thing to a seemingly irrelevant thing) and **imagery** (even subtle background images that harken back to a memory or a desired future).
- Example: Coca-Cola has sold "brown sugar water" for decades by associating it with family, friends, and good times — even people who never had that exact experience are brought back.
- When positive associations transfer (whether the person lived it, knows someone who did, or can imagine it), the offer is grasped more easily and therefore trusted more rapidly.

### Six Important Aspects Your Creative Should Have (Ch. 21)
Not all must appear in every creative, but **#1 always must**.
1. **Reflect your hook** (MANDATORY — must always exist).
2. **Tell a story** about how your offer can change or affect their life; carousel ads are great for this.
3. **Display the product** if you sell a physical product or can demonstrate it (works best further down the funnel).
4. **Stand out in the newsfeed** using contrasting colors, small video ads or moving GIFs — flashy but still on brand.
5. **Be on brand** using a simple style guide of consistent fonts and colors so people recognize your brand (brand lift/recognition matters even if it's a vanity metric).
6. **Play off emotions already associated with an image** — use icons/images people already have a relationship with (e.g. low iPhone battery, low bank account to convey scarcity).

### STEPPS — viral content principles (Jonah Berger, *Contagious*) — Three-Step Video Ad Formula chapter
Six principles that increase the chances and number of shares (not guarantees — they raise share probability; virality is science, not luck):
1. **Social Currency** — people share things that make them look good / part of an in-the-know group (illustrated by the secret NYC bar "Please Don't Tell").
2. **Triggers** — sights, sounds, and stimuli that remind us of related products and ideas.
3. **Emotion** — content inspiring high-arousal positive emotion (awe) or negative emotion (anger, anxiety) is more likely to go viral (Berger, 2012, *Journal of Marketing Research*).
4. **Public** — content built to be shared and enjoyed by everyone, not exclusive to the recipient.
5. **Practical Value** — relevant content that improves viewers' lives gets shared.
6. **Stories** — content with a beginning, middle, and end matches how we remember information and is more shareable.
- Real-world proof: Julius Dein grew from 0 to 6M+ Facebook followers in a year with funny, emotional, social-currency-loaded prank videos.

## Tactics & best practices

### Concepting: translate the hook into an image
- **Keyword-to-image extraction process (Ch. 21):** (1) Write the hook. (2) Pull the biggest keyword(s) out of it (e.g. "grade," "stack," "bottle," "build," "rock star," "guess"). (3) Think about what people associate with that keyword (grade → report card; stack → stacks of pancakes; build → ninjas building). (4) Type the keyword into Google or Dreamstime and see what comes up first. **Google works best** because its top images are the ones people have clicked on most for that keyword — let Google's dataset tell you the associated image rather than guessing.
- **Use literal imagery of the hook keyword (Ch. 21)** — simple, literal images repeatedly outperform: "work/life balance" = a gauge with work vs. life (enhance as a GIF with the needle moving); "don't bottle up your emotions" = sad bottles; "not all wounds are visible" = bandages; "build it for you" = ninjas building the app; "how does your metabolism stack up" = colored stacking showing progression; "rock star" = a band with marketing icons in the music notes; ad template library = image made to look like a library; "guess where your customers are hiding" = a Guess Who mockup.
- **Recreate a familiar interface/platform the audience uses daily (Ch. 21):** 60-Second Blog ad (one of DM's highest-converting ever) made the image look like WordPress + a clock for "60 seconds"; took ~20 min to design. Messenger ad mocked up to look like a Facebook-style message conversation (without copying FB exactly).
- **Borrow a culturally familiar meme/format and apply it to your niche (Ch. 21):** SamCart used the "what we're told it's like vs. what it's really like" meme applied to sales funnels — highly shareable because the overwhelmed target audience could relate.
- **Pattern interrupt to stop the scroll (Ch. 21):** arrange the product/elements in an unexpected pattern that makes the brain curious (wine-delivery example showed product in an unusual arrangement).

### Image/design execution
- **Make ads pop with a few complementary/contrasting colors (Ch. 21):** use only a few color choices; don't add ten different colors or it gets busy. Contrast helps the image stand out against the newsfeed background.
- **Use only one or two font families per image (Ch. 21):** too many fonts hurts readability (the Amy Porterfield example was hard to read because of the many fonts, not the black background).
- **Put hook text / CTA directly on the image (Ch. 21):** many people skip the ad copy and look straight at the image, so place a few of the most important hook words (a line of copy or a CTA button) on the image itself. Adding text onto a carousel image also helps it stand out.
- **Make ad creative congruent with the landing destination (Ch. 21):** the landing page design elements should match the ad for instant brand connection (ad scent). DM keeps ads similar to the featured images on their blog so viewers instantly recognize the brand.
- **When using people in imagery, direct their gaze at the viewer or the CTA (Ch. 21):** viewers follow the subject's gaze. The Jimmy Buffett ad had him looking directly at the viewer; if a person looks elsewhere, viewers look there too — so point the gaze at whatever you want clicked.
- **Display physical products in a non-boring way (Ch. 21):** add an eye-catching element rather than a plain product shot (makeup brush with pink blush coming off it; Survival Life waterproof-lighter video with rain falling while it stays lit, a penny, and a CTA; Wag retargeting ad using dogs + "50% off your first walk" baked into the image). Some markets (e.g. beauty) require really high-quality design.
- **Prefer designed/branded images over photographic stock (Ch. 21):** photo-style stock blends in with friends'/family's photos and gets buried. The Kabbage carousel used stock photos; DM's version of the same concept used their cartoon brand style with on-image text and stood out more.

### Working with designers
- **Designers are not marketers — own the marketing instruction (Ch. 21):** designers make pretty pictures, but a pretty picture isn't always a high-converting creative. You supply the marketing thinking. Outsource via Design Pickle (unlimited designs, can be expensive) or hire an in-house designer (the best thing that happened to DM's ads). Don't tell the designer what colors/fonts to use — that's their strength; your job is to ensure all marketing elements are present.
- **Designer brief / Google-research workflow (Ch. 21):** (1) Google the concept and screenshot images you like into a document. (2) Do rough drawings of what you envision so the designer doesn't just clone the found image. (3) Use the **Ad Grid** (Perpetual Traffic podcast ep. 33) to map avatars × hooks; per hook, generate ~3–5 image ideas. (4) Always have the designer create one "wildcard" of her own — the more she does, the better she gets (some now beat the marketer's). (5) Send the doc, get a first draft. (6) Walk through every element live so she understands your vision. (7) Iterate — sometimes 3–5 revisions, sometimes perfect on the first try.
- **Continuously study ads (Ch. 21):** creative is not a one-time system. Use AdEspresso's Facebook ad library (studied almost daily); screenshot ads in your own feed and circulate good/bad examples with the team (DM keeps a Slack room sharing ads daily to educate designers). Avoid relying on Snagit or Fiverr for serious ad creative.

### Building & placing the ad
- **Five Format options (Ch. 28):** Carousel, Single Image, Single Video, Slideshow, Canvas.
- **Image sourcing — three choices (Ch. 28):** upload a new image, reuse a previously uploaded image, or use Facebook's free stock images; follow the Recommended Image Specs when uploading.
- **Add Instagram placement during setup (Ch. 28):** under Page and Links, attach an Instagram account so the ad runs across both placements.
- **Always use the Call to Action button (Ch. 28).**
- **Test variations from the order-confirmation window (Ch. 28):** after confirming an ad, reuse the same window to spin up additional ads testing different images or copy — the fast path to multiple creative variations in one campaign.
- **Like-ad creative options (Ch. 11):** single image, single video, or slideshow; the recommended Like-ad image size is an unusually wide **1200 × 444** (not the standard 1200 × 628). If you don't supply an image, Facebook defaults to your cover photo; free stock images are available via "Free Stock Images."
- **What images catch attention (Ch. 11):** images that clash with Facebook's white/light-blue scheme (solid black or bright backgrounds), black-and-white images, and close-ups of faces.
- **Landing View setting for Like ads (Ch. 11):** controls where users go if they click the headline instead of Like (timeline, likes, or photos). **Timeline is usually best** — so keep good content on your timeline.
- **Use Canva for cover photos (Ch. 9):** pre-sized templates correctly sized for profile picture and cover photo dimensions (http://www.canva.com).

### Video
- **Add captions/subtitles in Power Editor (Three-Step Video Ad Formula chapter):** under Ads → Video Captions, choose auto-generate, upload an SRT file, or no captions. Captions are "recommended" because viewers with sound off can still read the message. Don't rely on auto-generation — strong regional accents cause errors; creating the text yourself lets you control size and background color so it stands out.

### Scaling & social proof
- **Reuse the original Post ID to consolidate social proof (Five-Tier Scaling System):** every ad has both an **Ad ID** and a **Post ID**. The Post ID refers to the content inside the ad — two ads with identical copy/creative still get unique Post IDs and are treated as separate posts. Nine duplications of a winning ad set create nine new posts (ten total) to moderate, thinning social proof across all of them. Solution: use the **same Post ID** (the original ad's) in all duplicates; the Ad ID differs per ad, but the shared Post ID collects all comments/likes/shares in one place, and Facebook rewards favorable reactions with more impressions at lower cost.

## Checklists

### Ad Creative Checklist — 6-step designer thought process (Ch. 21)
- [ ] **Create a powerful message and conceptualize images** — take the hook, research, search Google and competitors; figure out the offer's benefits, who you're targeting, the end result the customer receives, and the emotions/feelings to convey.
- [ ] **Sketch out ideas before laying out design elements** — don't worry about colors or fonts yet; ensure all the marketing elements are in the image.
- [ ] **Use complementary or contrasting colors** — Facebook ads must pop; use only a few color choices (not ten — it gets busy).
- [ ] **Choose your fonts** — use only one or two font families for readability.
- [ ] **Add text and a CTA to your image** — many people skip the copy and go straight to the image; put a few of the most important hook words (a line of copy or a button) on it.
- [ ] **Make sure your image is congruent with your brand** — be consistent; the landing-page/destination design elements should match the ad so viewers instantly recognize the brand.

### Steps to build a Single Image ad (Ch. 28)
- [ ] After creating your ad set, click **Continue** to reach the ad creation page.
- [ ] In **Format**, choose one of the five formats (Single Image for this walkthrough).
- [ ] Under **Images**, upload a new image, reuse a previous one, or use free stock images (follow Recommended Image Specs).
- [ ] Under **Page and Links**, select your Facebook page; add an Instagram account if you have one.
- [ ] Enter your **Website URL** and ad copy.
- [ ] Add a **Call to Action** button.
- [ ] Click **Review Order**.
- [ ] Click the green **Place Order** button so Facebook reviews the campaign, ad set, and ad.
- [ ] Click the blue **Continue** button to finish.
- [ ] After confirmation, create a new ad in the same window to test different images or copy.

"We're excited you visited the site, we're sad you didn't purchase, but

## Pitfalls to avoid

- **Don't use eyeball-bait imagery (puppy / pretty woman) (Ch. 21):** an unrelated attractive image only grabs attention and generates unqualified clicks from people who don't care what's behind it. Ad scent breaks once they land. If the image isn't conveying the hook, you're just catching eyeballs.
- **Don't get lazy and pull a random stock image (Ch. 21):** the cat-hook MLM ad used a stock cat at a keyboard with no relation to network marketing — costly, terrible for the brand, and a magnet for unqualified clicks. The creative is the biggest part of your ad; don't get lazy here.
- **A custom image with no marketing thought is as bad as a stock cat (Ch. 21):** the "no-hook bland-graph" agency ad ("here's how we help our clients generate a strong ROI...") gave no reason to care, and its graph image didn't match the copy and was meaningless. Go through the thought process of conveying the message in the image or it blends into the newsfeed.
- **Photographic stock images get buried (Ch. 21):** they blend in with friends'/family's photos. Use designed/branded imagery (and add on-image text) to stand out.
- **Too many fonts kills readability (Ch. 21):** limit to one or two font families.
- **Too many colors makes the ad busy (Ch. 21):** use only a few complementary/contrasting colors.
- **Don't rely on the designer for the marketing (Ch. 21):** designers make pretty pictures, not necessarily high-converting ones — you must supply detailed marketing direction.
- **Don't rely on Facebook's automatic caption generator for video (Video Ad Formula chapter):** strong regional accents cause transcription mistakes; control the captions yourself.
- **Don't leave duplicated winning ads on separate Post IDs (Five-Tier Scaling System):** it thins social proof across many posts and multiplies community-management work — reuse the original Post ID.
