# strategy_mindset

> The foundational mindset, mental models, and named frameworks for winning at Meta (Facebook/Instagram) ads — treating advertising as a complete selling system, mastering high-leverage fundamentals first, and matching message + offer to audience awareness. Source: *Ultimate Guide to Facebook Advertising (3rd ed.)*.

## Core frameworks

### Facebook Flight Plan (the book's organizing mental model)
A former airline pilot's metaphor: profitable Facebook advertising is learned like flying — through training, systems, SOPs, checklists, and redundancy. The book is structured as a "Facebook Flight School" with key lessons, normal procedures/checklists (routine operation), abnormal procedures/checklists (troubleshooting), and flight-planning guides. Mindset: it takes time, energy, great training, and quality experience, but once learned it's "like riding a bike." (Ch. "Your Facebook Flight School")

Core philosophy: master fundamentals before complexity — you start in a single-engine Cessna/Piper with an instructor, not a Boeing 747. Rookies get caught up in complexities and possibilities instead of mastering basics. Best early advice: **keep it simple and gain experience**, then layer on advanced strategies. (Ch. "First Two Stages of the Facebook Flight Plan")

The Flight Plan stages:
1. **Stages One & Two** — Build the foundation. By the end you should have built: a Like Campaign, a Link Post Ad, a Short-Copy Ad, and a Long-Copy Ad. (Ch. 14)
2. **Stage Three** — Entered only after **1,000+ conversions** (lead, registration, or sale) at an acceptable cost/ROI, having already tested hooks and offers. Adds two layers on top of the existing structure: (1) Video Ads and (2) Retargeting Strategies for Hot Prospects. Dynamic Product Ads (DPAs) are introduced at the very end. Purpose: turn cold traffic into warm audiences and warm audiences into customers. It's a model/guide, not a rigid prescription. (Ch. 15)

Milestone note: generating your first **100+ conversions** is the hardest part of the whole journey (like the first two minutes of a shuttle launch before booster separation); once past it the path gets easier. Conversion counts are guidelines, not rigid rules. (Ch. 14)

Why layer on more ad types: any single audience contains people at different **awareness levels, intent levels, trust/rapport levels, and buyer personas** — all needing different messaging, and many people must see your ads repeatedly before acting. (Ch. 14)

### UPSYD — the five levels of audience awareness
A Facebook-friendly reversal of Eugene Schwartz's five levels of product awareness from *Breakthrough Advertising* (1966). Schwartz's original levels (Most Aware → Product Aware → Solution Aware → Problem Aware → Unaware) are reversed into the acronym **UPSYD** (pronounced "upside"). (Ch. 13)

- **U = Unaware** — don't even realize they have a problem.
- **P = Problem Aware** — recognize the problem but don't know what to do about it.
- **S = Solution Aware** — know solutions exist but not which is right, and don't know your specific product.
- **Y = Your Solution Aware** — know your product but haven't purchased yet.
- **D = Deal** — just need a great deal to purchase.

Use UPSYD to diagnose why past campaigns failed, fix them, and decide exactly who to target and how to message them. The goal of Facebook ads is to **perpetually move people up the staircase** from awareness to intent.

**Where to start:** Begin at **Level 3 (Solution Aware)**, build momentum, then expand outward identifying the best messaging for each level. Targeting only "Your Solution Aware" people (e.g., people who already know Intermittent Fasting) gets sales easily but can't sustain a business long-term — the audience is too small. (Ch. 13)

Applied to cold traffic: an educational video ad takes prospects "from a state of unawareness all the way up the UPSYD ladder" before they hit the sales page (educate → elevate status → present product as the next logical solution). (Ch. 17)

### Customer Buying Cycle (the subconscious emotional process)
Every purchase follows this process regardless of what's being bought (Ch. 13):
1. **Problem** — a problem you want solved (not necessarily a catastrophe; e.g., looking good for a reunion).
2. **Research / Interest** — depending on market knowledge, you research (Google, Amazon reviews, ask friends).
3. **Evaluate options** — done emotionally, not logically ("how will this make me feel?").
4. **Justify with logic** — "the mind will justify what the heart has already decided" (Roy H. Williams).
5. **Purchase.**
6. **Brand Ambassador** — if they love it they become a raving fan and tell others (to help others AND to further justify their own decision). This is where you strive for all customers to end up.

### Three Business Categories (match the selling system to the category)
Nearly all Facebook advertisers fall into one of three categories, each needing a *different* selling system because they differ in offer, inventory, pricing, margins, purchase frequency, competition, costs, and the level of trust required for the next-step ask. (Ch. 12)
1. **Retail, Local, and Service Businesses**
2. **eCommerce and Physical Products**
3. **Digital and Information Products** (including SaaS)

Trust ladder by ask size: downloading a cheat-sheet (low trust) → free chiropractic assessment requiring travel + appointment (medium) → pulling out a credit card for a $70 bottle of powder (high). The big mistake: most businesses copy a model built specifically for *information marketers* — but with interruption marketing there is **no one-size-fits-all funnel**. Identify your category, use the proven system for it, then tweak. (Ch. 12)

### 7 Power Questions (when copy is ready)
Run a set of seven "power questions" before finishing any copy/ad. You don't need all seven — nailing even **one or two** effectively makes your marketing far more powerful and surfaces hooks you previously missed, and beats most competitors. The ideal is to answer all seven well. Caveat: this amplifies attention, so the underlying product/service must actually be good. (Ch. 20)

### Four Definitions of an Ad "Working"
"Is my ad working?" has four progressively deeper measures; Facebook directly answers only the first (via CTR / clicks). (Ch. "A Few Fundamentals")
1. Does the ad encourage users to **click**?
2. Will users do the **next step** you want after clicking?
3. Will users ever **purchase**?
4. Will users **stop buying from the competitor**?

### Audience-First (James) vs You-First (Ricky) — the twins fable
Two opposing mindsets dramatized as identical twins. (Ch. 4)
- **Ricky (you-first):** showcases his own 10,000-hour mastery, leans on referrals + discounts, frames mastery as a daunting climb requiring talent. Result: admiration but zero conversions; struggling business. (Day-trader father = short-term profit outlook.)
- **James (audience-first):** teaches a quick shareable win (four chords ≈ any song), lets the prospect DO it and succeed, frames mastery as achievable shortcuts, offers a low-friction lead magnet. Result: mile-long waiting list, 12 trained instructors, instant opt-ins. (Architect mother = whole-system long-term vision: "one small thing at the very beginning of a process map can exponentially affect the end result"; conductor father = fit all pieces together.)
- **Lesson:** Make the **audience the star/hero**, not yourself. Don't impress people with how great YOU are; make the prospect feel capable. "He wasn't the star of the show — his audience was the star." Be like James.

## Tactics & best practices

**Mission & economics**
- **Core mission:** buy a click for $1, turn it into $2, then make more profit from that $2 than competitors do. Requires: understand your sales funnel, craft a compelling ad, a focused landing-page goal, and automatic tracking/follow-up of leads and customers. (Ch. 1)
- **The grinding mindset:** restated, reliably and repeatedly turn a $1 click into $0.01 of pure profit, then scale volume. Like pro gamblers "grinding" a 1% edge round after round. "Empires are built on a 1 percent net profit." Don't despise a reliable penny — just make a lot more of them by getting more clicks. (Ch. 1)
- Facebook reframed as a **targeted, pay-for-performance billboard**: an electronic billboard shown only to your exact audience for free, where you pay ~$1 only when someone takes action — far more efficient than untargeted traditional media (a real billboard cost ~$10,000/month). (Ch. "Your Facebook Flight School")

**Foundational priorities**
- **Master the selling system, not just the ad platform.** After $60M+ in spend across 100+ accounts, the #1 lesson: it's about mastering the whole selling system that begins inside Facebook, not the platform mechanics. Treat the ad platform as one piece of a larger funnel/offer/conversion system. (Read This First)
- **Don't live inside Ads Manager / Power Editor when starting.** The leverage ("small hinges swing big doors") is in strategy, audience awareness, offers, and creative — not in mastering every dial. (Ch. 2)
- **Getting the offer/messaging right is THE key** — the single most important takeaway. Without a winning offer/message you can't scale no matter how much of a "Facebook ninja" you are; tactics like DPAs are secondary. (Ch. 15)
- **Strategy & messaging beat targeting & ninja optimization.** Even with perfect targeting you can't know a prospect's top-of-mind problem state. Facebook is really about getting messaging right — understanding the conversations in people's minds and meeting them where they are. (Ch. 13)
- **Right audience + great hook/offer = a one-two punch.** Per Perry Marshall: selling to the RIGHT PERSON beats all sales/copy/negotiation techniques (the wrong person has no money, doesn't care, won't be persuaded). A great hook/offer can win even with sloppy targeting; tightening targeting on top super-charges it. (Ch. 23)
- **Don't get caught up on retargeting** — hook and offer matter most; retargeting is just "icing on top." Profitable campaigns run ~90% cold traffic, ~10% retargeting. (Ch. 17)
- **Match value/trust to the size of the ask.** Minor ask (cheat-sheet opt-in) = simple image link post, short copy, video optional. Bigger ask (webinar 90–120 min) = pre-commit with a short clip + long copy. Highest ask (free+shipping requiring a credit card; product sales page) = strong upfront value via an educational video that elevates prospect status first. (Ch. 12, 17)

**Provide value, drive awareness, build intent**
- Core formula principle (from "provide value in advance"): transform users who are unaware of the problem and your brand into people who are (1) problem-aware, (2) solution-aware, and (3) have intent to purchase — via a staged funnel, not a cold "buy now." (Ch. "Your Facebook Flight School")
- **Intent vs. Interruption:** Google/YouTube searchers show active intent, so direct solution ads work (e.g., "We fix broken water pipes, there in 30 minutes"). Facebook is **interruption marketing** — no one is looking for a solution; they want an escape. The same plumbing ad bombs on Facebook. AdWords skills don't carry over. (Ch. 13)
- **One good ad can power the whole system** — simultaneously generating fans, leads, and customers while building brand goodwill, and making it much easier to scale without conversion costs skyrocketing. A winning long-copy ad can later become the storyboard for a video ad. (Ch. 2, 3)

**Build momentum first**
- **Just start** — boosting a post is the low-friction first step. "You don't have to be great to start, but you have to start to be great." (Zig Ziglar). (Ch. "Facebook Easy Button")
- **Get quick momentum before optimizing strategy** — use Facebook's "easy button" rather than designing the perfect strategy out of the gate, or you'll get overwhelmed. Refine strategy later. (Ch. 5)
- Apply the **80/20 / accelerated-learning** approach — master the few high-leverage fundamentals first (like learning the 4 most popular guitar chords to play almost any song), which also makes advanced parts easier later. (Ch. 2)
- **Flight-training focus:** like a student pilot focusing on 3 controls (yoke, rudders, throttle) and ~4 instruments, look "outside the airplane" — master a handful of core controls before chasing every advanced feature. (Ch. 2)
- The **ad-creation foundation determines your ceiling**: "The ceiling of your advertising profit, scalability, and the amount of wasted money swallowed up by Facebook, is directly dependent on the quality of ad creation foundation you build." It's an acquired skill (training + practice + tools + system + guide), not raw talent. (Ch. 2)

**Like campaigns as the beginner's "flight simulator"**
- Low-risk learning: no landing pages, thank-you pages, conversion pixels, or tracking needed. Build it in Power Editor for hands-on practice + real data (audience reactions, interest performance, desktop vs mobile, right column vs newsfeed). (Ch. 11)
- Three benefits beyond education: (1) **Social proof** — friends-of-fans targeting; ads look recommended/popular (the "restaurant with a line" effect). (2) **Fans see your ads ~2× more** — non-fans capped at 2 newsfeed views/day, fans up to 4/day (right-column has no frequency cap). (3) **Build warm audiences** — conversion campaigns to quality fans yield the best ROI. (Ch. 11)

**Hooks & research**
- **Research is the key to a great hook** — don't copy competitors outright, but don't ignore them (a huge source of ideas). Ogilvy spent 3 weeks on the Rolls-Royce manual; the Mercedes work increased U.S. sales 400% in one year (10,000 → 40,000 cars). If the greatest ad man spends 3 weeks, you can spend a few hours. If fulfillment falls short of the hook's promise, the hook stops working. (Ch. 16)
- **Four research methods to hack the hook:** (1) Google, (2) Surveys, (3) Interviews, (4) Facebook. (Ch. 16)
- **Survey the hook** — use open-ended, specificity-forcing questions to find hidden patterns (Bob's mail-order-bride business found 40% were truckers; authors found ~40% of customers were consultants/agencies → spawned a certification program). Use SurveyMonkey. (Ch. 16)
- **Interview the hook** — narrow to your BEST avatar (Who is your customer? Which is your best? Why?), then interview that group directly (e.g., inside a relevant Facebook group) about fears, desires, and ideal solutions. Solution-elicitation question: "If there was a solution to this problem, what would it look like?" (Ch. 16)

**Video & copy**
- **Long-term video ads beat short-term image/direct-response ads.** Image ads are a "sprint" that only works on already-primed buyers and dies in ~7–10 days ("a race for fools" — Roy H. Williams). Video ads are "the steps that make up the marathon" — like radio/TV, they run profitably for 6 months+ (some agency client videos for 1–2 years+). Goal: a "set it and forget it" campaign. Does NOT require high production value. (Ch. "Three-Step Video Ad Formula")
- **Guard-up vs guard-down entry:** image-only leads arrive with guard up, expecting to be sold; video-first leads arrive guard down, pre-sold, with "your brand in their hearts and minds." Always ask before launch: "How is that person coming into our sales funnel?" (Ch. "Three-Step Video Ad Formula")
- **Start now, improve later** — the most vital action is to *make a video*; first efforts are never perfect but each improves your ideas (which matter most). An iPhone mic is broadcast-quality enough. (Ch. "Three-Step Video Ad Formula")
- **Focused attention is the prerequisite for conversion** — don't assume people will read/watch. The job of copy is to make your brand/message focal and salient; if they don't pay attention, it won't convert regardless of product quality. (Ch. 20)
- **Environment reset (Pavlov's "new room"):** trained dogs stopped salivating at the bell in a new room — changing environment causes a mental reset. Moving a person through a "portal" makes them more likely to make a new decision (cited via Cialdini's *Pre-Suasion*). (Ch. 20)
- **Creative = being a good marketer.** Always ask: "How can I convey a marketing message that will get someone to do what I want them to do?" (Recommended: Elizabeth Gilbert, *Big Magic*.) (Ch. 21)

**Operate like a professional**
- **Treat Facebook ads as a profession, not a hobby** — done well it's a "$1,000-per-hour skill." 90% of advertisers merely dabble, making it an expensive tax write-off. (Ch. 1)
- **Live in the "is" world, not the "should be" world** — accept how people actually behave (95% in mild "hypnosis" chasing Likes/Shares) and use it. (Ch. 1)
- **Working ≠ playing on social media.** Be the chef who samples the soup, not the diner. Inject creativity, then measure and track. Never confuse activity with productivity. (Ch. 1)
- **The Founder's Rule:** bet on a business succeeding as long as a dynamic, visionary founder is at the helm (e.g., Zuckerberg → plan on Facebook staying competitive). (Ch. 1)
- **Reach is the whole ecosystem** — Facebook's ad tools are the gateway to Instagram, WhatsApp, Messenger, and Oculus communities. (Ch. 1)
- **Checklists reduce costly mistakes** — per Atul Gawande's *The Checklist Manifesto*, hospitals adopting airline-style checklists had dramatically lower error rates. Build checklists/SOPs into ad operations for repeatable, scalable results. (Ch. "Your Facebook Flight School")
- **Maintain a living Ad Creation SOP**, updated monthly, because the platform changes constantly. (Ch. 28)
- **Goals → Content → Targeting sequence:** pick the business goal first, then find matching content, then choose targeting when you boost. Measure organically, stack worthy posts ($1/day × 7 days), then spend more on winners. (Ch. "More Boosted Posts")
- Almost **every business should advertise on Facebook** (B2C and B2B) — an estimated **80–90% of your customers have Facebook accounts**. (Ch. 5)

## Checklists

### Eliminate Facebook distractions to protect productivity (Ch. 1)
- [ ] Delete the standard Facebook app from your phone (also Twitter, LinkedIn, etc.).
- [ ] Realize the world is in a never-ending conspiracy to rob you of time, attention, creativity, and mental space.
- [ ] Close the Facebook tab in your browser.
- [ ] Entirely stop using Facebook in the usual fashion during work hours.
- [ ] Block all email notifications from Facebook and other social media.
- [ ] Halt ALL smartphone notifications from every social media app — no banners, no red badges.
- [ ] When you do need Facebook, use the phone *browser* instead of the app.

### Pre-flight diagnostics before any campaign (Ch. 13, 14, "Three-Step Video Ad Formula")
- [ ] Identify which of the three Business Categories you fall into and use that category's proven selling system.
- [ ] Map your target audience across the UPSYD awareness levels.
- [ ] Decide where on the UPSYD staircase to start (default: Level 3, Solution Aware).
- [ ] Match the value/trust in your ad to the size of the ask.
- [ ] Ask: "How is that person coming into our sales funnel?" (guard up vs guard down).
- [ ] Run the 7 Power Questions on the copy before finishing the ad.

## Pitfalls to avoid

- **No one-size-fits-all funnel.** Most businesses wrongly copy a model built for information marketers; interruption marketing has no universal solution — match the system to your Business Category, then tweak. (Ch. 12)
- **Copy-and-paste / paint-by-numbers templates burn out fast.** They may work briefly but fade and require constant rotation. Aim to build a "masterpiece" and to become an architect/engineer who understands *why* it works — skills that transfer to any channel. (Ch. 2)
- **Over-focusing on targeting data / hyper-targeting & retargeting** is the biggest mistake; it makes people miss the big picture. Strategy and messaging win. (Ch. 13, 17)
- **Don't obsess over ad/ad-set counts, image/copy versions, or audience size** before building the foundation. (Ch. 2)
- **Don't run the same direct-intent ad you'd run on Google.** A solution-specific plumbing ad bombs on Facebook because no one there is looking for a solution. (Ch. 13)
- **Don't buy cheap bulk likes off-Facebook** — a high ratio of non-engaging fans kills organic reach (Like-campaign Myth #3). Sloppy/broad targeting also produces poor-quality leads (Myth #2). (Ch. 11)
- **Advertise responsibly** — "with great power comes great responsibility." Mastery of persuasion at scale obligates you to avoid manipulative or harmful offers. (Ch. 3)
- **Don't expect overnight mastery or treat ads as set-and-forget to learn.** Running profitable, scalable campaigns is genuinely hard (the author says harder than learning to fly); commit to systematic learning and experience-building. (Ch. "Your Facebook Flight School")
